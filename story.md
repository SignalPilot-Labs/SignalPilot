# The Last Transmission

The signal came in at 3:47 AM, too weak to be noise and too structured to be natural. Dr. Maren Solís had been alone at the observatory for six months when she heard it — a repeating sequence of prime numbers folded into the background radiation of a dying star. She sat very still in the cold blue glow of her monitors and listened to it three times before she trusted herself to breathe.

She ran every check she knew. The dishes were calibrated. The filters were clean. The software had no known bugs that would produce this pattern. By dawn she had filled two notebooks with calculations, and by noon she had concluded what she had been afraid to conclude from the start: the signal was not from this solar system, and it was addressed to no one but her.

The addressing was the strangest part. Buried in the sequence, encoded in a base-12 mathematical language she had to reconstruct on the fly, was a coordinate — not of a star or a planet, but of the chair she was sitting in. Its latitude, longitude, altitude, and something she had no name for that she suspected was a timestamp. The origin of the signal predated the observatory by three hundred years.

Maren spent a week deciding what to do. She could report it, and watch the finding get absorbed into committees and press releases and arguments about funding. She could stay silent, and carry it alone. In the end she did neither. She wrote back. She aimed the observatory's transmitter at the source coordinates, encoded her reply in the same base-12 language, and sent a single sentence: *We are still here.*

The star collapsed four days later, a quiet white dwarf death that her instruments recorded without ceremony. The signal never came again. But Maren kept her notebooks, and on nights when the sky was clear she would sit in her cold chair, surrounded by the static of the universe, and feel — with more certainty than she could explain — that the silence was not empty.
